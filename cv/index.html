Teaching

Humanities 106: Life Stories

Illinois Institute of Technology: Fall 2009, Spring 2010, Fall 2010, (Fall 2011)

A course covering the different genres of writing (autobiography, biography, memoir, creative non-fiction) that tell individuals' stories. Different iterations of my section have included readings from Benjamin Franklin, Harriet Jacobs, Frederick Douglass, Ernest Hemingway, Jack Kerouac, Martin Luther King Jr., Tim O'Brien, Dave Eggers, and David Sedaris. In addition to introducing literary research to freshman students, this course covers historical context and philosophical concepts that allow students to engage texts critically through written work, supplemented by instructor and peer feedback at all stages of the writing process.

Literature 309: Short Fiction

Illinois Institute of Technology: Spring 2010

This course featured selections of less frequently anthologized short fiction pieces, as well as a few classics. The goal of the course was to introduce students to this genre, collectively formulate the fundamental tenets, explore how the evolution of this genre over the past two centuries, and question why short fiction is still a relevant and an integral part of media we consume today. For their final projects, I encouraged students to go beyond writing a term paper and several students developed collages, photography portfolios, and websites/multimedia content.

Literature 380: Shakespeare: Print, Screen, and Stage

Illinois Institute of Technology: Fall 2010

A literature class where students read Shakespeare and compared the text of his plays to film and stage performances, including a trip to the Chicago Shakespeare Theater. Students performed close readings of the plays and examined contextual material to gain insight into Shakespeare's writing. This course also included sections on the vocabulary and theory of film and stage criticism, with an emphasis on applying those concepts to both traditional and interpretive films and performances.

Literature 315: The Novel

Illinois Institute of Technology: Spring 2011

This course focused on the historical progression of the novel as a genre from the proto-novels of the 18th century through postmodern and contemporary titles. I provided audio feedback on response papers (in the form of .mp3 files) and we preformed in-class research and undertook a collaborative Wikipedia editing exercise.

Literature 380: Hard Times

Illinois Institute of Technology: Spring 2011

A look at historical class conflicts as portrayed in literature. Students read and researched novels concerning times of economic difficulty and political upheaval, and worked in teams to research contextual information on the time periods we read about. I concluded the semester with a look at the the global financial crisis and economic recession.

Conferences and Publications

The Impact of Emerging Literacies on Instant Messaging and Supplemental Writing Instruction

Computers and Writing: 5/21/10, Purdue University

A mini-workshop where participants briefly simulated a writing center tutorial conducted through a web-based instant messaging application (Google Docs) in order to gain an understanding of the complexities of the literacy that has emerged from this media and how that literacy expands and reshapes writing instruction.

Open Source Tutoring: An examination of the Role of Service-Learning in the Writing Center

Chicagoland Writing Center Association Conference: 2/6/10, DePaul University

In this piece I introduced a concept called "Open Source Tutoring," a method of tutoring that both disseminates knowledge amongst peer tutors and reveals the processes of writing to tutees, preparing both parties to engage in reflexive and informed writing practices while also demystifying supplemental writing instruction. Community outreach initiatives are often forced to reduce the tutoring process to a brief encounter (e.g. students visit the writing center with a college application personal statement for fifteen minutes on a college visit day). Using open source practices (i.e. teaching students how to replicate the experience of providing writing consultation with their peers) and open source technology (e.g. free and open applications for writing) along with long-term engagement strategies helps maximize the impact of supplemental writing instruction outside of the university community.

Community, Collaboration, Cogitation: The Three "C's" of Tutor Training

Chicagoland Writing Center Association Conference: 2/7/2009, North Park University

A review of progressive tutor training methods developed at the DePaul University Center for Writing-based learning, including the theoretical underpinnings for structured approaches to writing center administration: structuring time for professional development, community building, formal/informal collaboration, and critical reflection on tutoring approaches. Coauthored with Laura Friddle, Rickie-Ann Legleitner, and Alexis Maloof.

"The Decline of Writing?"

DePaul Magazine: Spring/Summer 2009

An article that critically examines and debunks the myth that technology negatively impacts student writing ability. Technology in fact provides a multitude of opportunities to write that were not present in the past. Please note that although the article appears in a "question and answer" format, the magazine editor added those questions as section headers; the authors did not "respond" to the questions in the article when writing the article. Coauthored with Lauri Dietz and Alexis Maloof.

"Hot Topics In Writing" Podcast

A podcast exploring issues in teaching and tutoring writing. Episodes included interviews with faculty members at DePaul as well as topical segments related to student writing. All episodes written, produced and recorded by myself, Alexis Maloof, and Julie Glasier. Available on iTunes.
